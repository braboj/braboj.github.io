## The field

Branimir started as an automation engineer at Solvay Sodi, one of Bulgaria's largest chemical plants. Not the kind of job where you sit in front of a screen — the kind where you bail water by hand to keep PLCs alive during a flood, climb 180-metre chimneys in wind, crawl through narrow tunnels, walk into an ammonia cloud, work on radioactive equipment on Christmas night, and witness the destructive power of a multi-tonne steam head punch through a metre-thick concrete wall.

He automated compressors, utilities, and process units on both Honeywell and Siemens hardware, ported Honeywell control blocks to Siemens SCL, and cleaned up after consultants who left systems broken, endangering human life and equipment. He learned what industrial software actually costs when it fails — and what it feels like when it works. There is nothing quite like watching a unit you built from scratch come alive for the first time and run exactly as designed. That feeling never gets old.

When things went wrong, people would say: *"If Branko is here, everything will be all right."* That is the standard everything since has been built to meet.

## The SDK

At Hilscher, Branimir moved into software and discovered Python. His first project worked. The second worked better. Then he hit a gap that no existing library could fill: protocol stacks built for communication, not for testing. You cannot craft malformed packets. You cannot simulate edge cases the spec does not cover.

That gap is where Imbra Connect began. The SDK grew to cover DeviceNet, CAN, Modbus, OPC-UA, and MQTT — with packet crafting and adversarial testing as first-class features. Brutal American deadlines. Impossible milestones. All delivered. The model was lean: developer and tester working in parallel, every artifact shipped already tested. No handoffs. No surprises at the end of the sprint. The approach worked so well the company adopted it across other projects. Branimir trained six engineers and earned the nickname *the bastard from heaven*.

## The historian

At Heidelberg Materials, Branimir worked on PxTrend — an industrial historian built by Paranext. When the setup stalled, he did what came naturally: he drove to the nearest plant in Devnya, talked to the operators, and learned the product directly from its creator. Results followed. The understanding of what a historian should do — and what it rarely does — became the foundation for ImBrain.

## The company

Imbra.Soft was founded after Branimir's daughter was born and the answer became obvious: build it himself.

Every product — the protocol SDK, the historian, the control block library — comes from fifteen years of plant floors, firmware, and knowing firsthand what it costs when the software fails. The complexity is ours to solve.
